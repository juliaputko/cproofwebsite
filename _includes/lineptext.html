C-PROOF has been collecting high-resolution oceanographic data along the “Line P” glider line since 2019 with the support of our partners at Fisheries and Oceans Canada . Our ocean gliders allow us to collect data without the use of a ship, reducing costs and our carbon footprint, and can collect data even during the roughest weather conditions. Line P crosses the Northeast Pacific from the southern tip of Vancouver Island in Canada to Ocean Station Papa, approximately 1500 km to the west. It is one of the longest running ocean monitoring transects in the world. The origins of Line P can be traced back to December 1949, when it was operated as an ocean weather station. Ocean Station Papa was occupied by ‘weatherships’ as part of the Weathership Program until 1981, after which Fisheries and Oceans Canada (DFO) began running the Line P Program. Now DFO runs 3 yearly cruises with 27 stations along the transect, sampling physical properties such as temperature and salinity, as well as ecologically-relevant properties such as dissolved oxygen and nutrients. The Northeast Pacific ocean environment is undergoing rapid changes. Climate change is having a profound effect on ocean ecosystems. Between 2014 and 2017, a marine heatwave observed in the Gulf of Alaska (“The Blob”) increased the ocean temperatures dramatically, reducing oxygen levels and nutrients and impacting numerous marine species including salmon, which generally benefit from cool, well-oxygenated water. As the ocean continues to warm, there could be a significant shift in fish migration pathways, an increase in harmful algal blooms, and many other damaging effects to Northeast Pacific ocean ecosystems. The need for monitoring has never been more critical! After being deployed near Tofino off the west coast of Vancouver Island, our gliders travel across the narrow continental shelf and out into the open ocean, diving repeatedly from the surface to a depth of 1000 m. On each mission, a glider can travel the entire length of Line P, to Ocean Station Papa and back again, covering around 3000 km over-ground in several months. Their data, combined with DFO sampling and other measurements such as satellite altimetry and ocean colour, will give us a more complete picture of the important physical and ecological processes in this region, and help us address the ongoing challenge of our changing climate.”
